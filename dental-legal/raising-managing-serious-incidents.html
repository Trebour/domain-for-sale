Raising and Managing Serious Incidents in Dentistry

Practical guidance for UK dentists on identifying, reporting, and managing serious incidents while meeting GDC and CQC obligations.

What Is a Serious Incident in Dentistry?

A serious incident in dentistry refers to an unexpected or avoidable event that results in, or has the potential to result in, significant harm to a patient. These incidents require structured reporting, investigation, and learning to protect patient safety and meet regulatory expectations.

Serious incidents may involve clinical errors, safeguarding failures, consent breaches, data protection issues, or systemic practice failures.

Why Serious Incident Reporting Matters

Properly raising and managing serious incidents is a professional and regulatory obligation. The General Dental Council (GDC) and Care Quality Commission (CQC) expect dental professionals to demonstrate openness, accountability, and learning following adverse events.

Failure to report or manage serious incidents appropriately can escalate matters into GDC investigations, CQC enforcement action, or dental negligence claims.

Examples of Serious Incidents in Dental Practice

  • Wrong tooth extraction or irreversible treatment error
  • Failure to diagnose serious pathology
  • Significant consent failures
  • Data breaches involving patient records
  • Safeguarding concerns
  • Repeated clinical record-keeping failures

Immediate Steps After a Serious Incident

When a serious incident occurs, patient safety must always be the first priority. Immediate clinical care should be provided to address harm or prevent further risk.

Practices should preserve records, document events accurately, and avoid altering clinical notes retrospectively. Early transparency and factual documentation are essential.

Duty of Candour

UK dental practices are subject to a statutory duty of candour. This requires dentists to inform patients when something has gone wrong, explain what happened, apologise appropriately, and outline steps being taken to prevent recurrence.

Failure to comply with the duty of candour can itself become a regulatory issue.

Internal Reporting and Investigation

All serious incidents should be reported internally using the practice’s incident reporting procedures. A structured investigation should follow, focusing on root cause analysis rather than individual blame.

Learning outcomes should be documented and shared with the team to support continuous improvement.

External Reporting Obligations

Depending on the nature of the incident, practices may need to notify external bodies, including:

  • The Care Quality Commission (CQC)
  • Indemnity providers
  • The GDC (in limited circumstances)

Understanding when external reporting is required is critical to avoiding regulatory breaches.

Serious Incidents and GDC Risk

While not all serious incidents lead to GDC action, poor handling, dishonesty, or repeated failures can trigger fitness to practise investigations.

Consistent, professional, and transparent management significantly reduces regulatory risk.

Supporting Staff and Wellbeing

Serious incidents can be distressing for dental teams. Practices should provide appropriate support, encourage open discussion, and avoid a culture of blame.

Learning and Prevention

The ultimate purpose of serious incident management is learning. Practices should implement changes, update protocols, and monitor outcomes to prevent recurrence.
