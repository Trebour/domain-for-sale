Raising and Managing Serious Incidents in Dentistry

Practical guidance for UK dentists on identifying, reporting, and managing serious incidents while meeting GDC and CQC obligations.

What Is a Serious Incident in Dentistry?

A serious incident is an unexpected or avoidable event resulting in, or potentially resulting in, significant harm to a patient. These require structured reporting and investigation to protect patient safety and meet regulatory standards.

Examples include clinical errors, safeguarding failures, consent breaches, data protection issues, or systemic practice failures.

Why Serious Incident Reporting Matters

Reporting serious incidents is a professional and regulatory obligation. Proper handling demonstrates accountability and supports learning, while failure can escalate to GDC or CQC investigations, or dental negligence claims.

Examples of Serious Incidents

  • Wrong tooth extraction or irreversible treatment error
  • Failure to diagnose serious pathology
  • Significant consent failures
  • Data breaches involving patient records
  • Safeguarding concerns
  • Repeated clinical record-keeping failures

Immediate Steps After a Serious Incident

Prioritise patient safety, provide clinical care, preserve records, and document events accurately. Avoid retrospective alterations of clinical notes.

Duty of Candour

UK dental practices must inform patients when something has gone wrong, apologise appropriately, and outline steps to prevent recurrence. Failure may lead to regulatory issues.

External Reporting Obligations

  • The Care Quality Commission (CQC)
  • Indemnity providers
  • The GDC (in limited circumstances)

Serious Incidents and GDC Risk

Poor handling, dishonesty, or repeated failures can trigger fitness to practise investigations. Professional and transparent management reduces regulatory risk.

Supporting Staff and Wellbeing

Provide support to dental teams, encourage open discussion, and avoid a culture of blame.

Learning and Prevention

Implement changes, update protocols, and monitor outcomes to prevent recurrence.
