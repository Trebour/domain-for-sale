Responding to Patient Complaints

Practical legal guidance for UK dentists on managing patient complaints, reducing regulatory risk, and avoiding escalation to the GDC.

Why Patient Complaints Matter

Patient complaints are a common and often unavoidable part of dental practice. While many complaints are minor and easily resolved, others can escalate into formal disputes, dental negligence claims, or General Dental Council (GDC) investigations if not handled correctly.

How a dentist responds to a complaint can significantly influence the outcome. A well-managed response may resolve the issue early, while a poorly handled complaint can increase legal, financial, and regulatory risk.

Common Reasons Patients Make Complaints

Dental patient complaints typically arise from a combination of clinical, communication, and expectation issues. Common triggers include:

  • Perceived poor clinical outcomes
  • Disputes over treatment costs or refunds
  • Alleged lack of informed consent
  • Communication breakdowns
  • Appointment delays or administrative errors
  • Concerns about professionalism or behaviour

The Dental Complaints Procedure (UK)

All UK dental practices are required to have a clear complaints procedure. This applies to both NHS and private practices.

The procedure should explain:

  • How patients can raise a complaint
  • Who handles complaints within the practice
  • Expected response times
  • How complaints are investigated and resolved

Initial Response: What Dentists Should Do First

The first response to a patient complaint is critical. Dentists should acknowledge complaints promptly, professionally, and without defensiveness.

Best practice includes:

  • Acknowledging receipt quickly
  • Thanking the patient for raising the issue
  • Avoiding admissions of liability
  • Maintaining a calm and respectful tone

What to Avoid When Responding to Complaints

Certain responses can significantly increase risk and should be avoided:

  • Ignoring or delaying responses
  • Responding emotionally or defensively
  • Blaming the patient or colleagues
  • Providing inconsistent explanations
  • Discussing the complaint publicly or online

When Complaints Escalate to the GDC

Not all complaints lead to regulatory action, but unresolved complaints may be escalated to the GDC by patients, employers, or third parties.

Once a complaint reaches the GDC, it becomes a formal fitness to practise matter. Dentists should treat this stage with particular care.

Complaints and Dental Negligence Claims

Patient complaints sometimes precede dental negligence claims. Statements made in complaint responses can later be used as evidence in legal proceedings.

Dentists should ensure consistency between complaint responses, clinical records, and any future legal correspondence.

Role of Indemnity Providers

Most dental indemnity providers offer support with complaint handling. Dentists should notify their provider early, particularly where there is a risk of escalation.

Support may include:

  • Drafting or reviewing responses
  • Legal advice
  • Representation if matters escalate

Record Keeping and Evidence

Clear, accurate clinical records are essential when responding to complaints. Records should support explanations given and demonstrate compliance with professional standards.

Protecting Your Practice

Effective complaint handling is not just reactive. Practices can reduce complaints by:

  • Setting realistic expectations
  • Improving communication
  • Training staff in complaint management
  • Regularly reviewing complaints trends
